The City of Evora

- Evora - Roman and Arabic architecture stand out in ancient buildings and imposing arcades, such as the Roman Temple. Medieval walls spread throughout the region, as well as the churches, some of them rich in gothic beauty.

- Evora OTE* is a Portuguese city, capital of the Evora district in the Alentejo region and Central Alentejo subregion, with 49,252 inhabitants in 2011. Evora is the only Portuguese city member of the Oldest European Cities Network.

- It is the seat of the fifth largest municipality in Portugal, with 1 307.08 km² in area and 56 596 inhabitants (2011), subdivided into 12 parishes. The municipality is bordered to the north by the municipality of Arraiolos, northeast by Estremoz, east by Redondo, southeast by Reguengos de Monsaraz, south by Portel, southwest by Viana do Alentejo and west by Montemor-o-Novo. It is the seat of the district and former diocese, being an ecclesiastical metropolis (Archdiocese of Évora).

- Its well-preserved historic center is one of the richest monuments in Portugal, which gives it the epithet of Museum City. In 1986, the historic city center was declared a World Heritage Site by UNESCO.

- It is known as the de facto capital of the Alentejo.

The Roman Temple of Evora

- The Roman Temple of Evora, mistakenly known as Temple of Diana, is located in the city of Evora in Portugal.

- It is part of the historic city center, which has been classified as a World Heritage Site by UNESCO. The Roman temple is classified as a National Monument by the DGPC. It is one of the most famous landmarks of the city and one of the most significant symbols of the Roman presence in Portuguese territory.

- Located in the parish of Sé and São Pedro, in Largo Conde de Vila Flor, it is surrounded by the Évora Cathedral, the Inquisition Court, the Lóios Church and Convent, the Évora Public Library and the Museum.

The Chapel of Bones

- It was built in the seventeenth century on the initiative of three Franciscan monks who, in the spirit of the time (religious counter-reform according to the norms of the Council of Trent), intended to convey the message of the transience of life, as the famous warning at the entrance: "We bones that are here for your waiting." In addition to the spiritual question, there was also a physical question, namely; there were forty-two monastic cemeteries in the Evora region that were taking up too much space and strategic places that many intended to use for other purposes. So they decided to remove the skeletons from the earth and use them to build and decorate the chapel.

- The chapel, built on the site of the early brotherly dormitory, is made up of three naves of 18.70 m long and 11 m wide, with the light coming in through three small cracks on the left.

- Its walls and eight pillars are "decorated" with bones and skulls linked by brown cement. The vaults are of white plastered brick, painted with allegorical motifs to death. It is a monument of a penitential architecture of ornate arches with rows of skulls, cornices and white ships. It was estimated around five thousand bones, including skulls, vertebrae, femurs and others, from cemeteries, located in churches and convents of the city, which were linked with brown cement and are arranged on the walls, ceiling, columns and even outside. There are still two whole skeletons hanging by chains on one wall, one of them a child.

- The chapel was dedicated to the Lord of the Steps, an image known in the city as Lord Jesus of the House of Bones, which impresses with the expressiveness with which it represents the suffering of Christ in his walk with the cross to Calvary.

Açorda Alentejana

- The basic composition of the breadboard is garlic, salt, olive oil, boiling water and sliced ​​bread, but aromatic herbs such as coriander or pennyroyal are added to this mixture and can be served with fresh fish (boiled or fried), cod or egg (poached or boiled).

- The cooking process involves stepping the salt with the aromatic herb and garlic, a mixture to which olive oil is added. Pour over the water where the accompaniment, still boiling, is boiled, and pour sliced ​​Alentejo bread.

- The Alentejo Açorda was one of the final candidates for the 7 Wonders of Portuguese Gastronomy.

Migas Alentejana

- Alentejo Migas are one of the best known dishes of Alentejo cuisine. Like the azordas and other dishes of this Portuguese region, the basic ingredient is bread, a traditional Alentejo product (formerly called the "granary" of Portugal, due to the production of cereals which, however, has been declining a lot). The meat used is pork, another regional product with great traditions.

- The bread to use should have the characteristics of traditional Alentejo bread - too much of the bread sold elsewhere, too fluffy and crispy, can give bad result.
